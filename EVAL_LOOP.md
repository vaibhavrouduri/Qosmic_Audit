The current eval loop is simple: generate an artifact-backed audit report, run `eval_report.py`, and use the result to decide whether the report is acceptable or needs revision. If the evaluator finds issues, `run_eval_loop.py` creates a targeted revision prompt. The agent can then revise the report without recrawling or adding new evidence. In the current version, this often pushes the report score close to 100, but that alone is not enough. A high evaluator score only matters if the evaluator itself is trusted.

That is why human feedback is collected on every eval result. The human reviewer can agree or disagree with the evaluator and leave a reason. Over time, this feedback becomes the main source of improvement. If humans repeatedly say the evaluator is too generous, too strict, or missing a certain kind of issue, those patterns should be turned into better evaluator rules, better scoring thresholds, or better report-generation instructions.

The first stage of autonomy is improving the evaluator. Once enough human feedback has been collected, the evaluator should become more aligned with human judgment. At that point, it can be trusted more as the gatekeeper. If a stronger, human-vetted evaluator consistently gives low scores, then the problem is probably not the evaluator anymore; it means the forward pass needs work. That could mean improving the crawler, changing the report-generation skill, tightening `AGENTS.md`, or changing how the agent selects and uses artifacts.

The system also stores useful traces: crawl artifacts, manifests, reports, eval JSON, revision prompts, revised reports, and human feedback. Over time, those logs can become a dataset. They can be used to test new evaluator rules, compare report versions, identify repeated failure modes, and possibly train or tune a future judge/helper model that suggests better evaluator checks or report-generation changes.

In 1–3 months, the goal is for humans to review fewer routine cases. Early on, humans review many reports and eval results. Later, humans should mostly handle edge cases: low-confidence audits, new storefront patterns, risky recommendations, or places where the evaluator and human judgment disagree. The human surface area should shrink because every correction should feed back into the evaluator, the crawler, or the report-generation harness.
